Section 1: Introduction

Who This Programme Is For

This programme is built for intermediate to advanced lifters (1+ years consistent training) who want to maximise muscle growth over a structured 16-week block. You should already be comfortable in the gym and familiar with the core movement patterns.

It is not a beginner programme. If you've been training less than 12 months, start with the free Foundation Plan at sebastian-fisher.com/free.

Prerequisites: You should be able to perform a full range-of-motion squat, hip hinge, overhead press, and pull-up (or lat pulldown at bodyweight equivalent) before starting this programme.

How to Use This Guide

  1. Read Sections 1–2 first. Understanding the periodisation model and RPE scale is essential.
  2. Follow the blocks in order. Each block builds on the previous. Do not skip ahead.
  3. Use the training logs. Print them or copy to a notebook. Track every session.
  4. Take deload weeks seriously. They are programmed — not optional.
  5. Check the exercise guide for form cues and substitutions before starting.
  6. Adjust nutrition to match the phase. Surplus should increase with volume.

The Arnold Split

This programme uses a 6-day Arnold split — the classic bodybuilding frequency that lets you train each muscle group twice per week with sufficient volume and recovery:

DaySplitPrimary Muscles
MondayChest & BackPecs, lats, rhomboids, traps, rear delts
TuesdayShoulders & ArmsAll 3 delt heads, biceps, triceps, forearms
WednesdayLegsQuads, hamstrings, glutes, calves, adductors
ThursdayChest & BackPecs, lats, rhomboids, traps, rear delts
FridayShoulders & ArmsAll 3 delt heads, biceps, triceps, forearms
SaturdayLegsQuads, hamstrings, glutes, calves, adductors
SundayRestFull recovery day
Why Arnold Split? Each muscle gets hit twice per week — the sweet spot for hypertrophy. Unlike PPL, chest and back are paired together, creating a natural push-pull superset dynamic and keeping sessions balanced. Shoulders & arms get a dedicated day, meaning no fatigue carryover from heavy pressing.

RPE & RIR Scale

All working sets use RPE (Rate of Perceived Exertion) to autoregulate intensity. RIR = Reps In Reserve.

RPERIRDescription
100Maximum effort. Could not do another rep with good form.
9.50–1Might have had one more, but not confident.
91Definitely one more rep left. Hard set.
82Two reps left. Challenging but controlled.
73Three reps left. Working weight, good form throughout.
64+Warm-up territory. Speed work only.
Common mistake: Most lifters train at RPE 9–10 every session and wonder why they plateau. This programme keeps most work at RPE 7–8, with RPE 9+ reserved for peak weeks and the final block. Trust the process.

Progressive Overload in Practice

Overload in this programme comes from three levers — not just adding weight:

  1. Volume: More sets per week as the block progresses.
  2. Load: Small weight increases week to week (1–2.5kg on machines/cables, 2.5–5kg on free weights).
  3. Effort: RPE creeps up from 7 to 9 across a block, even at the same weight.

Do not chase load at the expense of form. A slow, controlled cable fly at RPE 8 builds more muscle than a swinging 30kg dumbbell at RPE 10.

Mind-Muscle Connection & Tempo

Unless otherwise noted, use the following default tempo:

PhaseSecondsNote
Eccentric (lowering)2–3 secControl the negative. This is where damage occurs.
Stretch1 sec pauseBrief pause at full stretch position.
Concentric (lifting)1–2 secControlled but not explosive unless noted.
Peak contraction1 sec squeezeHard squeeze at the top where applicable.

Deload Philosophy: Heavy Compounds

Deload weeks in this programme are not "do nothing" weeks. Instead of dropping volume on isolation work, you swap the entire week to compound-focused, low-rep, heavy work:

  • Strip back to 4–5 exercises per session (all compounds)
  • Work up to heavy singles, doubles, and triples (RPE 8–9)
  • Total volume drops by ~50%, but intensity per set is high
  • This maintains neural drive and strength while letting connective tissue recover from high-rep isolation work
  • You'll often come back to the next block feeling stronger and more responsive to volume
Think of it this way: 3 weeks of bodybuilding → 1 week of powerlifting → repeat. The contrast is the recovery.

Block 3 — Peak Volume · Weeks 9–11

Highest volume block. This is where growth happens. RPE 8–9. Additional sets on all exercises. Intensity techniques introduced in Week 11.

This block is hard. Sleep 7+ hours. Eat in a surplus. If you're not recovering between sessions, reduce volume by 1 set per exercise rather than pushing through.

Key Changes from Block 2

  • +1 set on most exercises compared to Block 2 Week 7
  • Week 11 introduces intensity techniques on final sets: drop sets on machine work, myo-reps on cables, partials on isolation
  • RPE 8–9 across the board — you should be close to failure on most working sets
  • Total weekly sets per muscle group: 22–24

Block 4 — Intensity & Taper · Weeks 13–16

Volume drops back to moderate. Every set counts — intensity techniques on most exercises. RPE 9–10. Week 16 is a taper.

Block 4 Philosophy

You've spent 12 weeks building volume tolerance. Now you cash it in. Lower total sets but maximum effort per set. Every working set should be within 1 rep of failure. Intensity techniques are applied systematically.

Intensity Protocol by Week

After Week 16: Take a full deload week (compound heavy or complete rest), then you can either repeat the programme with higher starting loads, transition to a cutting phase, or begin a new training block.

Section 4: Exercise Guide

Chest & Back Day — Key Cues

Incline DB Press

Set bench to 30°. Bring dumbbells down until upper arms are parallel to floor or slightly below. Don't bounce. Press up and slightly in — dumbbells should nearly touch at the top. Squeeze pecs at top for 1 second.

Cable Chest Fly

Set pulleys at shoulder height or slightly above. Step forward for stretch. Arms slightly bent — locked elbow angle throughout. Bring hands together at chest level, squeeze 2 seconds. Control the return.

Machine Chest Press

Adjust seat so handles align with mid-chest. Press without locking elbows. Slow 2-sec eccentric. If machine has converging arms, use it — better chest activation.

Wide-Grip Lat Pulldown

Grip 1.5× shoulder width. Lean back 15°. Initiate by depressing shoulder blades, then pull elbows down and back. Lower the bar to upper chest. Control the return — feel the stretch at the top.

Seated Cable Row

Sit upright, slight forward lean at start for stretch. Pull to lower chest/upper abdomen. Squeeze shoulder blades together for 1 second. Don't use momentum.

Straight-Arm Pulldown

Stand facing cable, rope or straight bar at high position. Arms locked straight. Pull down in an arc until hands reach thighs. Feel lats stretch overhead on the return. Do not bend elbows.

Shoulders & Arms Day — Key Cues

Seated DB Lateral Raise

Sit on bench edge, slight forward lean. Raise dumbbells out to sides, leading with elbows (not hands). Stop at shoulder height. Lower slowly — 3 seconds down. Don't use momentum.

Cable Lateral Raise

Stand side-on to low cable. Cable runs behind legs. Raise arm to shoulder height, hold 1 second. Lower slowly. This provides peak tension at the top where DBs lose it.

Incline DB Curl

Set bench to 45°. Let arms hang straight down — full stretch on biceps. Curl without moving upper arms. Squeeze at top. Lower slowly — this is the money part of the movement.

Cable Rope Pushdown

Elbows pinned to sides throughout. Push down and split the rope at the bottom. Squeeze triceps for 1 second. Control the return — don't let the cable yank your arms up.

Legs Day — Key Cues

Leg Press

Feet shoulder-width, mid-platform. Lower until knees are at 90° or slightly below. Don't round lower back off the pad. Press through whole foot. Don't lock out knees at top.

Hack Squat

Narrow stance, feet low on platform for quad bias. Go deep — arse to ankles if mobility allows. Heels elevated on a plate if needed. Control the eccentric.

Lying Leg Curl

Point toes (plantar flexion) to reduce calf involvement. Curl fully — try to touch pad to glutes. Hold peak contraction 1 second. Lower slowly, don't let it drop.

Section 5: Nutrition

Caloric Surplus Guidelines

Building muscle requires a caloric surplus. Too much and you gain unnecessary fat. Too little and you leave gains on the table.

Training ExperienceSurplusExpected Gain
Beginner (< 1 year)300–500 kcal/day1–1.5 kg/month
Intermediate (1–3 years)200–350 kcal/day0.5–1 kg/month
Advanced (3+ years)150–250 kcal/day0.25–0.5 kg/month
Weigh daily, average weekly. If your weekly average isn't moving up at the rate above, add 100–150 kcal. If it's climbing too fast, remove 100–150 kcal. Adjust every 2 weeks.

Meal Timing & Training Nutrition

  • Pre-training (1–2 hrs before): Meal with protein + carbs. 30–40g protein, 50–80g carbs.
  • Intra-workout: Optional — 20–30g fast carbs (juice, gummy bears, cyclic dextrin) if sessions exceed 75 minutes.
  • Post-training (within 2 hrs): Protein + carbs. 30–50g protein, 50–100g carbs.
  • Before bed: Slow-digesting protein (casein, cottage cheese, Greek yoghurt).
  • Total meals: 3–5 per day. Spread protein evenly (30–50g per meal).
