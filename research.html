TRAINING · META-ANALYSIS

Volume and Hypertrophy: How Many Sets?

Krieger JW  ·  J Strength Cond Res  ·  2010  ·  PMID 20300012

Krieger (2010) analysed 8 studies comparing single vs. multiple sets per exercise. Multiple sets produced significantly greater hypertrophy than single sets across all populations studied.

Practical upshot: more sets equals more muscle, up to a recoverable threshold. Most naturals peak somewhere between 10–20 weekly sets per muscle group before recovery becomes the limiting factor rather than stimulus.

The implication for programming is clear: a single working set per exercise is not sufficient for hypertrophy in trained individuals. Volume needs to accumulate meaningfully across the week, structured to allow recovery.

TRAINING · RCT

Load vs Reps: Does It Matter for Building Muscle?

Schoenfeld BJ et al  ·  J Strength Cond Res  ·  2017  ·  PMID 27230820

Schoenfeld et al. compared low-load high-rep (25–35 reps) vs. high-load low-rep (8–12 reps) training across matched sets to failure. Both produced similar hypertrophy. The mechanism is the same: mechanical tension and metabolic stress.

Practical upshot: rep range matters significantly less than effort and total volume. Training hard across a wide rep range is more important than adhering to a specific hypertrophy rep window.

The “8–12 rep range” advice is not wrong — it’s just not the only valid approach. What matters is taking sets close to failure with sufficient total volume across the week.

TRAINING · REVIEW

Progressive Overload and Long-Term Adaptation

Peterson MD et al  ·  J Gerontol A Biol Sci Med Sci  ·  2011  ·  PMID 21068689

Peterson et al. conducted a meta-analysis on resistance training in adults. Progressive overload — increasing the demand placed on the body over time — was the primary driver of long-term strength and size gains.

Programmes without progressive overload produced significantly smaller adaptations regardless of exercise variety, volume, or other variables.

The body adapts to specific demands. If the demand doesn’t increase, the adaptation plateaus. This is why keeping a training log and tracking load, volume, and effort over time is non-negotiable for long-term progress.

TRAINING · META-ANALYSIS

Training Frequency: How Often Per Muscle Group?

Schoenfeld BJ et al  ·  Sports Med  ·  2016  ·  PMID 27102172

Schoenfeld et al. compared training a muscle once per week vs. twice or more. Higher frequency (2x/week or more) produced significantly greater hypertrophy when volume was equated.

Most of the old school literature matched this — every golden era programme hit muscles multiple times per week. The bro split (chest on Monday, back on Tuesday) is a recent and suboptimal invention.

The mechanism is likely related to muscle protein synthesis peaking and returning to baseline within ~48 hours. Spreading volume across 2–3 sessions per week keeps MPS elevated more consistently than one large session.

TRAINING · REVIEW

Deloading: Does Taking a Week Off Hurt Progress?

Ralston GW et al  ·  Sports Med  ·  2017  ·  PMID 28090050

Short periods of reduced training (1–2 weeks) following sustained volume blocks do not result in significant muscle loss in trained individuals. The fear of “losing gains” from a planned rest week is not supported by evidence.

Planned deloads outperform training to failure indefinitely. They allow full recovery of the nervous system and connective tissue, and performance in subsequent training phases is measurably improved.

Muscle tissue is maintained during short deloads. What’s lost is primarily glycogen and water. What’s gained is a recovered nervous system and connective tissue that can train harder when volume resumes.

NUTRITION · META-ANALYSIS

How Much Protein Do You Actually Need?

Morton RW et al  ·  Br J Sports Med  ·  2018  ·  PMID 28698222

Morton et al. meta-analysed 49 studies involving over 1,800 participants. Beyond approximately 1.62g/kg bodyweight, additional protein did not produce further increases in muscle mass in trained individuals.

People eating 1.6–2.2g/kg are covered. Bodyweight in grams (roughly 1g per lb) is a reliable, slightly conservative target that works for the vast majority of natural lifters.

Higher protein intakes (2.5g/kg+) are not harmful in healthy individuals, but the evidence does not support their superiority for muscle growth. Calories and training volume are likely more limiting factors for most people.

NUTRITION · REVIEW

Protein Distribution: Frequency of Meals

Areta JL et al  ·  J Physiol  ·  2013  ·  PMID 23459753

Areta et al. found that distributing protein over 4 meals produced greater muscle protein synthesis than the same total protein consumed in either 2 large meals or 8 small meals, over a 12-hour recovery period.

Practical upshot: 3–5 protein-containing meals across the day is more effective than front-loading or back-loading protein into one or two large meals.

Each meal should ideally contain at least 20–40g of protein to maximise muscle protein synthesis. Snacking on small amounts of protein throughout the day is less effective than structured meals.

NUTRITION · REVIEW

Dietary Fat and Testosterone

Hamalainen E et al  ·  J Steroid Biochem  ·  1984  ·  PMID 11524197

Low-fat diets (below roughly 15–20% of calories from fat) are consistently associated with lower testosterone in studies of both athletes and general populations. This finding has been replicated multiple times since the original 1984 study.

Saturated and monounsaturated fats appear most important for maintaining hormonal function. Very low-fat cutting diets can impair recovery and hormone production independent of calorie deficit effects.

Dietary fat should not be eliminated or severely restricted when trying to get lean. Keeping fat at a minimum of 20% of calories protects hormonal output during a deficit.

NUTRITION · ISSN POSITION STAND

Creatine: The Most Studied Supplement

Kreider RB et al  ·  J Int Soc Sports Nutr  ·  2017  ·  PMID 28615996

The International Society of Sports Nutrition 2017 position stand confirms creatine monohydrate as the most effective ergogenic nutritional supplement for increasing high-intensity exercise capacity and lean body mass during training.

Effects are modest but consistent and well-replicated across decades of research. Standard dose is 3–5g/day monohydrate. No loading phase is required for long-term use — saturation occurs over several weeks.

Creatine is one of the very few supplements where the evidence base is strong enough to recommend without qualification. It’s cheap, safe, and works. If you’re not using it, you’re leaving a marginal but real advantage on the table.

RECOVERY · REVIEW

Sleep: The Most Underrated Recovery Tool

Dattilo M et al  ·  Med Hypotheses  ·  2011  ·  PMID 21550729

Dattilo et al. reviewed the relationship between sleep deprivation and muscle recovery. Insufficient sleep increases cortisol, reduces growth hormone output, and impairs muscle protein synthesis — the three key recovery mechanisms.

Athletes sleeping fewer than 6 hours showed measurably worse recovery markers than those sleeping 8 or more hours. No supplement meaningfully compensates for consistent sleep deprivation.

Sleep is the recovery variable that training culture most systematically undervalues. Before spending money on recovery supplements, fix your sleep. The return on investment is not close.

RECOVERY · META-ANALYSIS

Exercise and Mental Health: The Evidence

Schuch FB et al  ·  J Psychiatr Res  ·  2016  ·  PMID 26907761

Schuch et al. conducted a meta-analysis of 25 RCTs involving over 1,400 participants. Both resistance training and aerobic exercise produced significant reductions in depressive symptoms compared to control conditions.

Effect sizes were comparable to pharmaceutical interventions in mild-to-moderate cases. Exercise is an evidence-based intervention for mood — not a supplement to medical treatment for severe cases, but a genuine primary intervention for many.

The implications go both ways: training improves mood, and poor mood reduces training adherence. This creates a positive feedback loop when the pattern is established, and a negative one when it breaks down.

RECOVERY · REVIEW

Gut Health and Exercise Performance

Clark A & Mach N  ·  J Int Soc Sports Nutr  ·  2016  ·  PMID 27317522

Clark & Mach reviewed the relationship between gut microbiome health, inflammation, and athletic performance. A compromised gut microbiome increases systemic inflammation, impairs nutrient absorption, and negatively impacts recovery.

High-fibre whole food diets with adequate probiotic exposure consistently outperformed processed-food diets on gut health markers. The gut is upstream of most performance variables.

You can eat 180g of protein a day and still not build muscle if your gut isn’t absorbing it. Gut health is not a wellness buzzword — it’s a genuine performance variable with a real evidence base.

RECOVERY · REVIEW

Stress, Cortisol, and Body Composition

Nakagawa S & Nakagawa T  ·  Cell Metab  ·  2017  ·  PMID 28326662

Chronic psychological stress elevates cortisol, which promotes abdominal fat accumulation, impairs insulin sensitivity, and suppresses anabolic hormone output including testosterone and growth hormone.

Stress management interventions produce measurable improvements in body composition independent of diet and training changes. High stress is a legitimate barrier to progress — not a soft or peripheral variable.

The implications for coaching: a client under severe chronic stress will not respond normally to training and nutrition. Addressing the stressor is part of the programme, not separate from it.

RECOVERY · META-ANALYSIS

Vitamin D and Muscle Function

Beaudart C et al  ·  J Clin Endocrinol Metab  ·  2014  ·  PMID 24932806

Beaudart et al. meta-analysed 30 studies on vitamin D and muscle outcomes. Deficiency — common in the UK given limited sunlight exposure for much of the year — is associated with reduced muscle strength and increased injury risk.

Supplementation at adequate doses improved strength outcomes in deficient individuals. Given the UK population is broadly deficient for six or more months of the year, 1,000–4,000 IU/day is genuinely justified without blood testing first.

This is one of a small number of supplements where blanket recommendation makes sense for a UK population. Cheap, safe, and directly relevant to both strength and general health outcomes.

TRAINING · REVIEW

Blood Flow Restriction (BFR) Training

Loenneke JP et al  ·  Acta Physiol Hung  ·  2012  ·  PMID 22849837

Loenneke et al. reviewed the evidence for blood flow restriction training — applying a cuff or wrap to partially restrict venous outflow during exercise at loads as low as 20–30% of 1RM. Some studies show hypertrophy comparable to traditional higher-load training.

The proposed mechanism is metabolic stress: the restricted blood flow causes lactate and metabolite accumulation, which may trigger growth factor release and cell swelling. The evidence base is growing but practical application — cuff placement, pressure, duration — is still being standardised.

Interesting as a research area, particularly for injury rehab (low load on damaged joints). Not a standard coaching method here — included as an area worth watching rather than a proven protocol.

TRAINING · ANECDOTAL + THEORY

Fascia Stretching While Pumped

Schleip R et al  ·  J Bodywork Mov Ther  ·  2012  ·  PMID 22703740  ·  + anecdotal

Schleip et al. explored fascial plasticity — the idea that fascia (the connective tissue sheath surrounding muscle) responds to mechanical load and can remodel over time. Fascia contains contractile cells (myofibroblasts) and behaves differently to passive connective tissue.

The bodybuilding hypothesis: stretching a fully pumped muscle maximises the pressure inside the fascial sheath, potentially promoting expansion and creating more room for long-term growth. This was popularised by Frank Zane and Lee Haney, who performed extended stretches between sets.

Formal evidence is thin. The mechanism is plausible, and the downside of stretching between sets is essentially zero. Filed as “worth doing, not proven” rather than a guaranteed hypertrophy tool.

RECOVERY · RCT

Sun Exposure, Vitamin D, and Testosterone

Pilz S et al  ·  Horm Metab Res  ·  2011  ·  PMID 21154195

Pilz et al. conducted a randomised controlled trial in which men supplemented 3,332 IU of vitamin D per day for 12 months. The vitamin D group saw significant increases in total testosterone, bioactive testosterone, and free testosterone compared to placebo.

Beyond supplementation, morning sunlight exposure supports vitamin D synthesis in the skin, entrains the circadian rhythm, and influences cortisol patterns throughout the day. Getting outside in the morning is a low-cost intervention with compounding hormonal effects.

This sits alongside the physical activity data: lifestyle behaviour outside the gym matters. Morning sunlight and daily movement are not wellness fluff — they have measurable effects on the hormonal environment in which training takes place.

RECOVERY · REVIEW

Physical Activity, Movement, and Hormonal Health

Kumagai H et al  ·  Eur J Appl Physiol  ·  2016  ·  PMID 26914484

Kumagai et al. examined the relationship between habitual physical activity and testosterone in healthy men. Regular physical activity — defined broadly, not just structured gym sessions — was consistently associated with higher testosterone and lower resting cortisol.

Walking, outdoor activity, and non-exercise activity thermogenesis (NEAT) contributed independently of structured training frequency. Sedentary periods between gym sessions were associated with worse hormonal profiles than active recovery days.

The practical message: the 23 hours per day outside the gym matter. Getting up, walking, leaving the house, staying physically active in an unstructured way all feed into the hormonal environment that makes training effective.
