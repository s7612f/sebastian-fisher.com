ARNOLD'S GOLDEN ERA SPLIT

At his peak, Arnold trained twice per day, 6 days per week — what we now call a "double split." Monday/Wednesday/Friday: chest + back in the AM, legs in the PM. Tuesday/Thursday/Saturday: shoulders + arms AM, calves + abs PM. Sunday: off.

Total sets per week: Approximately 350–400 sets. Modern lifters do 80–120. The difference? Muscle maturity, years of base building, and frankly — the pharmaceutical support that made recovery possible at that volume.

What you can extract:
1. High frequency (hitting each muscle 3x/week) works — just not at that volume for most.
2. Compound first, isolation to pump. Always.
3. Progressive overload plus volume accumulation. Both matter.
4. Train hard enough that every set counts, not just enough sets.

The takeaway isn't to copy the volume — it's to understand that frequency and compound movements built those physiques, not machines and isolation-only splits.

FRANK ZANE: QUALITY OVER QUANTITY

Zane trained on approximately 12–14 exercises per session, with 3–4 sets each. Lower volume than Arnold, but with intense focus on contraction quality, posing practice, and muscle-mind connection.

His core principles:
1. Pose between sets — it built awareness and helped with conditioning.
2. Calves every day. Non-negotiable.
3. Nutrition precision over volume: he ate clean and tracked macros before it was mainstream.
4. Rest/Pause training for lagging parts — intensity over volume.

If you're chasing aesthetics over mass, Zane's approach is more relevant than anything from modern bodybuilding. His 3x Olympia wins proved you don't need to be the biggest to be the best.

PLATZ ON LEGS

Platz squatted high bar, deep, with full range of motion — often for sets of 20–50 reps at weights that would floor most people. His leg day typically ran 2–3 hours.

Core session structure:
— Squat: worked up to max weight, then high-rep sets (20, 30, 50 reps)
— Leg extension: giant sets, peak contraction held
— Leg press: additional volume after squats
— Leg curl: 5–6 sets, strict form
— Sissy squat finisher
— Calves: 8–10 sets, standing and seated

The lesson isn't to train for 3 hours. It's that full ROM, compound movement dominance, and genuine intensity over long periods of time builds muscle that can't be faked.

THE PROBLEM WITH MODERN FITNESS ADVICE

The three biggest failures of modern gym advice:

1. Overcomplicating nutrition. Flexible dieting, intuitive eating, IIFYM, carb cycling — none of these work better than simply eating enough protein and maintaining a consistent calorie target. The complexity sells courses. It doesn't build muscle.

2. Avoiding heavy compound movements. The squat, deadlift, bench press, and overhead press built every great physique in the golden era. They're still the most effective tools. The trend toward machines and isolation "for safety" produces weaker, less developed physiques.

3. Chasing variety over progression. Progressive overload — adding weight, reps, or sets over time — is the mechanism of muscle growth. Changing exercises every week prevents progression. Old school lifters ran the same movements for years. That's why they were huge.

EATING OLD SCHOOL

Golden era bodybuilders lived on: eggs, beef, chicken, rice, potatoes, oats, milk. That's about it. High protein, moderate carbs, low processed food. Not because it was trendy — because it worked, it was cheap, and you could prep it fast.

The practical rules:
1. Protein at every meal. Aim for your bodyweight in grams per day.
2. Carbs around training. Less important when not training.
3. Cook in bulk once or twice a week. Don't make food complicated.
4. If it comes in a packet with more than 5 ingredients, it's not food.
5. Drink water. A lot of it.

The difference between a 3,000kcal day and a 3,500kcal day is roughly one extra meal of rice and chicken. That's the difference between cutting and bulking. You don't need an app for that.
