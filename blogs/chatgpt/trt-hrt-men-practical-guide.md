Information only, not medical advice. TRT (testosterone replacement therapy) is for men with symptoms of hypogonadism and consistently low testosterone confirmed by bloodwork. It is not a shortcut to muscle. If sleep, diet, and training are poor, fix those first.

1) Baseline Assessment
- Two fasted morning draws (07:00–10:00), separate days.
- Labs: Total T, Free T (calc or equilibrium dialysis), SHBG, LH/FSH, prolactin, oestradiol (sensitive), haematocrit/haemoglobin, full blood count, CMP, lipids, TSH ± fT3/fT4 if indicated.
- Rule out reversible drivers: sleep apnoea, excess weight, certain meds, shift work, heavy alcohol.

2) Starting Protocol (typical)
- 100–120 mg/week testosterone enanthate or cypionate.
- Split E3D–EOD injections. No routine aromatase inhibitor.
- Re‑test at 6–8 weeks (steady state). Judge by symptoms + labs, not numbers alone.

3) Monitoring & Adjustments
- Target Free T in a physiologic high‑normal range; track SHBG — low SHBG often benefits from smaller, more frequent injections.
- Oestradiol (E2, sensitive): manage by dose/frequency first; only consider AIs if clinically indicated.
- Haematocrit: if >54%, address before titrating further; consider donation if appropriate and clinician agrees.
- BP, lipids, sleep, glucose: foundations first; TRT is additive to lifestyle, not a replacement.

4) Fertility & Special Cases
- If preserving fertility, discuss hCG or enclomiphene under clinician supervision.
- Very low SHBG: consider ED/EOD micro‑dosing to reduce peaks/troughs; very high SHBG: slightly larger, less frequent dosing may be reasonable.

5) Red Flags & Contraindications
- Unmanaged sleep apnoea, prostate cancer history without specialist oversight, haematocrit above safe range, uncontrolled hypertension — address before or avoid therapy. Always work with a competent clinician.
