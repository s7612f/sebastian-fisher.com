The Vince Gironda Method — Old School That Still Works

Vince Gironda trained Monroe, Schwarzenegger, and Larry Scott before anyone else had heard of them. He ran a gym in North Hollywood from 1948 to 1997. He called himself the Iron Guru. His ideas about diet and training were decades ahead of the peer-reviewed literature. Most of them still hold up.

Here’s what he actually taught, stripped of the hagiography:

THE NECK PRESS

Gironda prescribed the barbell bench press to the neck rather than the chest, with a wide grip and elbows out. The idea was to take the triceps and anterior deltoid out of the movement and force the pecs to do the work. He believed the standard chest press left too much room for arm drive. He wasn’t wrong — most people who “bench press” are pressing with their front delts and triceps and calling it a chest workout.

The neck press is uncomfortable at first. The bar path feels wrong because it is — wrong for a standard press. But the stretch on the pecs at the bottom is significantly greater, and if you can train yourself to use it as a pec isolation movement, the development responds. It’s not a beginner exercise. Master the standard bench first.

THE DRAG CURL

Standard barbell curls allow the elbows to swing forward, which loads the front delts and reduces the peak contraction on the biceps. Gironda’s drag curl keeps the bar dragging up the torso with elbows tracked behind the body rather than out in front. This enforces strict bicep loading through the full range.

It’s objectively a more mechanically honest curl than the standard version. It’s also harder because you can’t cheat. Use less weight than you think you need.

8×8 TRAINING

Gironda popularised 8 sets of 8 repetitions with minimal rest — typically 30 seconds between sets. He called it the honest workout. The accumulated lactate is brutal. The metabolic stress on the target muscle is significant. Modern hypertrophy research has validated high-density training as a driver of muscle growth. Gironda was running this protocol in the 1950s.

The catch: the weight has to be genuinely challenging for 8 reps, and 30-second rest periods are shorter than most people can actually sustain when lifting seriously. Most people are resting 90 seconds and calling it 30. Time your rest periods if you try this. It will change the workout.

LOW-CARB BEFORE LOW-CARB WAS POPULAR

Gironda ran his clients on what he called the steak and eggs diet — red meat and eggs, little else. No carbohydrates for extended periods except strategic refeeds. He used this protocol for contest prep. His clients came in lean without the flat, depleted look that calorie restriction alone produces.

The mechanism wasn’t understood then the way it is now — what he was doing was driving down insulin, increasing fat oxidation, and keeping amino acid availability high from dietary protein. The result: fat loss without the corresponding muscle breakdown. His intuition was correct; the biochemistry supports it.

WHAT DIDN’T HOLD UP

Not everything he said was correct. He claimed the squat was bad for the glutes and that deadlifts built an unaesthetic physique. He dismissed the barbell row. Some of this was personal preference elevated to principle, and it shouldn’t be taken as doctrine. The compound movements he dismissed are still the foundation of any serious programme.

Use what works. Leave what doesn’t. That’s the only honest approach to training knowledge, then or now.

Gut Health Is Your Foundation

You can eat 180g of protein a day and still not build muscle if your gut isn’t absorbing it. Gut permeability, chronic inflammation, and a disrupted microbiome all degrade nutrient absorption — and most gym nutrition advice skips this entirely.

Absorption happens in the small intestine via enterocytes — specialised cells that transport nutrients across the gut wall. These cells have a rapid turnover: they live and die in 3–5 days. Their function depends on the integrity of the gut lining, the composition of the gut microbiome, and the absence of chronic inflammatory pressure. When any of those is compromised, absorption drops — for protein, for micronutrients, for everything.

LEAKY GUT IS REAL

Intestinal permeability — popularly called leaky gut — refers to increased passage of undigested food particles, bacterial toxins, and other substances through the gut wall into systemic circulation. This triggers an immune response. That immune response produces systemic inflammation. Chronic systemic inflammation impairs virtually every hormonal and metabolic process that training depends on.

The term became associated with alternative medicine, which caused mainstream nutrition to dismiss it. That dismissal was a mistake. The underlying biology is not contested — it’s in peer-reviewed gastroenterology. The controversy is about degree and clinical significance, not about whether intestinal permeability affects health. It does.

THE MICROBIOME AND PROTEIN UTILISATION

The gut microbiome produces enzymes that assist in the breakdown of dietary protein into absorbable amino acids. A disrupted microbiome means less enzymatic activity, less complete protein breakdown, less amino acid availability — even at identical protein intake. You can track every gram and still come up short if the gut isn’t working.

Fermented foods — kefir, kimchi, sauerkraut, plain yoghurt — provide live bacteria that support a diverse microbiome. This is not pseudoscience. Human clinical trials show measurable changes in microbiome composition and inflammatory markers from regular fermented food consumption. The mechanism is simple: diversity in the microbiome correlates with better metabolic outcomes.

WHAT ACTUALLY DISRUPTS THE GUT

Excess alcohol. Directly increases gut permeability. A Saturday night out followed by a heavy Sunday training session is working against itself at the gut level.

Chronic stress. Cortisol elevation alters gut motility, reduces barrier integrity, and shifts microbiome composition toward inflammatory species. Sleep is directly relevant here — it’s when cortisol drops and the gut repairs.

Processed food dominance. Not because “processed food is bad” as a vague claim, but because low-fibre diets starve the gut bacteria that produce short-chain fatty acids. Short-chain fatty acids — particularly butyrate — are the primary fuel source for colonocytes (the cells lining the colon). No fibre, no butyrate, no healthy gut lining.

NSAIDs. Ibuprofen, aspirin, naproxen — all increase gut permeability with regular use. If you’re training hard and using NSAIDs routinely to manage soreness, you are undermining your own recovery at the gut level.

PRACTICAL PRIORITIES

Hit 30g+ fibre daily from whole food sources. Include fermented foods regularly. Protect sleep. Limit alcohol to what you’re actually willing to accept the cost of. Use NSAIDs acutely, not chronically. These are not complex interventions. They’re prerequisites for your protein intake to do what you’re paying for it to do.

Why I Train Low-Carb (And It Doesn’t Kill My Lifts)

Around 50g of carbohydrates per day as a baseline. Strength where I want it. Recovery fine. The claim that carbohydrates are non-negotiable for resistance training performance doesn’t match the evidence or the lived experience.

I’m Type 1 diabetic. Managing blood sugar around training is not optional for me in the way it is for most people. Carbohydrates — even complex, “clean” carbs — require careful insulin management around sessions, create glycaemic variability that affects energy levels and cognitive function, and make post-workout recovery nutrition more complicated. Going low-carb simplified all of this substantially.

WHAT THE EVIDENCE ACTUALLY SAYS

Most of the research showing performance deficits from low-carb diets involves endurance athletes. The mechanism is clear: glycolysis is the primary pathway for sustained moderate-to-high intensity aerobic work. For resistance training, the picture is different. Resistance exercise relies primarily on the phosphocreatine system and glycolysis for acute ATP production, but the glycogen requirement per session for typical hypertrophy training is substantially lower than endurance work.

Multiple studies have compared low-carbohydrate and standard-carbohydrate diets in resistance-trained individuals and found no significant difference in strength or hypertrophy outcomes when protein is matched. The key variable is protein. If you’re hitting 2g/kg bodyweight in protein, your muscle protein synthesis is not carbohydrate-limited.

THE ADAPTATION PERIOD

This needs to be said clearly: the first two to four weeks of reducing carbohydrates significantly are rough. Performance drops. Fatigue is higher. Sleep may be disrupted. This is real and it’s the reason most people abandon low-carb before the adaptation is complete.

The adaptation involves upregulation of fat oxidation enzymes, increased mitochondrial density, changes in substrate selection during exercise, and changes in electrolyte regulation. Until these adaptations are in place, you are running on a system that hasn’t been calibrated yet. The four-week mark is approximately when things stabilise. Most people quit in week two.

MY CURRENT APPROACH

50g of carbohydrates per day as a floor. On heavy training days — squat or deadlift sessions — I’ll use a targeted carbohydrate approach: 30–50g of fast-acting carbohydrate consumed pre-session. This gives a glycolytic substrate spike without the all-day glycaemic management that higher carbohydrate eating requires.

Protein is high — 200g+ daily. Fat is the primary calorie source otherwise: eggs, red meat, butter, olive oil, whole dairy. This is functionally Gironda’s steak and eggs protocol adapted for modern context.

Overhead Press went from 40kg to 60kg during my last recomp block running this protocol. That’s not a coincidence.

Food Combining — The Forgotten Principle

Food combining got associated with early-century naturopathy and was dismissed wholesale when evidence-based nutrition arrived. That dismissal was probably too thorough. The core principle on enzyme pathways holds up, even if the extremist versions of the theory don’t.

WHAT THE ORIGINAL THEORY CLAIMED

The Hay diet and related food combining systems claimed that proteins and starches should never be eaten in the same meal because they require different digestive environments — protein requiring acid (pepsin), starches requiring alkaline (amylase). The claim was that combining them produced poor digestion of both.

The extreme version of this is demonstrably false. The digestive system handles mixed macronutrient meals without difficulty — it secretes multiple enzyme types simultaneously. You don’t need to eat steak and rice at separate meals. The body manages.

WHAT ACTUALLY HOLDS UP

The more nuanced version is about macronutrient pairing effects on glucose response and insulin signalling. Fats consumed with carbohydrates slow gastric emptying and blunt the glycaemic response. This is well-documented and clinically relevant for diabetics in particular. Protein with carbohydrates produces a different insulin response than carbohydrates alone.

For training purposes, this matters in a practical way: a high-glycaemic carbohydrate consumed with fat will be absorbed more slowly — which is not always desirable around training, where rapid glucose availability may be the goal. Understanding how macronutrient combinations affect absorption rate and insulin response lets you use nutrition more precisely.

THE PRACTICAL APPLICATION

Pre-workout: If you’re using targeted carbohydrates before a session for glycolytic fuelling, consume them without significant fat. Fat slows absorption and may delay the glucose spike you’re aiming for.

Post-workout: Protein is the priority. The insulin response from protein alone is sufficient for driving amino acids into muscle tissue. Adding a large carbohydrate bolus post-workout doesn’t significantly increase MPS over protein alone at adequate dose.

General: Eating protein with every meal is the single highest-leverage food combining principle. It manages glucose response, provides amino acid availability, and supports satiety. Everything else is secondary.

Adherence Is the Programme

The number one predictor of training results is not the quality of the programme. It’s whether the person follows it consistently over a meaningful period of time. This isn’t controversial — it just doesn’t sell programmes.

My own training data makes this uncomfortable to look at. Before my last recomp block: 2.8 sessions per week on average. Twenty-two sessions where I left a gap of three or more days. One stretch of 63 days where I trained almost nothing. I had good programmes. I had a gym membership. I had knowledge. I had gaps in adherence that made most of it irrelevant.

WHY PEOPLE DON’T ADHERE

The programme is too ambitious. A five-day-a-week programme for someone with a history of training three days a week is going to fail. Not because five days is wrong, but because the jump is too large. The principle of progressive overload applies to frequency just as it does to load. Increase gradually.

The programme doesn’t match the life. Programmes designed in a vacuum — assuming 90-minute sessions, access to a full gym, optimal sleep and nutrition — fail the moment life disrupts any of those things. A programme built around your actual constraints will outlast one built around ideal conditions.

Motivation is being used as fuel instead of systems. Motivation is a feeling. Feelings change. The session must happen on days when the feeling isn’t there — which is most days. The people who train consistently don’t feel more motivated. They’ve built the session into the structure of the day so the decision doesn’t need to be made fresh each time.

WHAT ACTUALLY DRIVES ADHERENCE

Fixed session time. Same time every day. Before work is structurally superior to evening because the decision is made before the day has accumulated enough friction to derail it. This is not preference — it’s data from adherence research across exercise populations.

Programme simplicity. A programme you can execute without thinking — where the exercises, sets, and reps are automatic — removes cognitive load from the session. Cognitive load at the gym is energy you’re not putting into the training.

Minimum viable session standard. The session happened if you turned up and did the work, regardless of whether it was the best session you’ve ever had. A bad session is infinitely better than a missed session. The athlete who trains badly 5 days a week will outperform the athlete who trains brilliantly 2 days a week over any meaningful timescale.

THE RECOMP SHIFT

When I locked in for the 6-week recomp block, the change wasn’t primarily in the programming. The programming was similar. The change was in the decision structure. I treated sessions as non-negotiable commitments rather than options. In 36 days I had one gap over 2 days. The results from those 36 days exceeded the results from the previous 320.

The most important piece of training advice is the most boring: show up. Consistently. Over time. Everything else — periodisation, exercise selection, rep ranges — is secondary to that.

How to Cut Without Losing the Gym

Standard advice: eat less, do more cardio, accept the strength drop. That framing puts the deficit at the centre and treats training as a fat-burning tool. That’s the wrong priority order. Muscle preservation comes first. The deficit is a consequence, not the method.

WHY PEOPLE LOSE MUSCLE WHEN CUTTING

Three mechanisms: caloric deficit large enough to reduce anabolic hormone production, protein intake insufficient to match the increased catabolic pressure from a deficit, and training volume and intensity reduced due to perceived fatigue. The third one is the most controllable and the most often mismanaged.

When you reduce training load because “you’re cutting”, you remove the primary signal for muscle retention. The body has no reason to maintain muscle mass that isn’t being used to produce force. The stimulus has to stay. If anything, training intensity should increase during a cut — the volume might drop slightly but the mechanical tension on the muscle must remain high.

THE DEFICIT PARAMETERS THAT MATTER

Size of the deficit. 300–500 kcal below TDEE is the sustainable range. Larger deficits accelerate fat loss in the short term but increase catabolism, reduce training performance, and are harder to maintain consistently. The research on this is clear: gradual deficits produce better body composition outcomes than aggressive ones.

Protein intake. Higher than during a bulk. 2.2–2.5g/kg bodyweight during a cut. The increased catabolic pressure from a deficit requires increased dietary amino acid availability to counter it. Most people are eating less protein when cutting — calories are down, food volume is down, protein volume drops with it. Track it.

Cardio: type and timing. Low-intensity steady-state cardio (walking, cycling, swimming) does not significantly impair strength or muscle mass when programmed correctly. High-intensity interval training concurrent with resistance training in the same sessions does create interference at the cellular level. If you’re running intervals immediately before heavy squats, you’re compromising the squat session. Separate them — different sessions, different days where possible.

THE DELOAD

Every fourth week, reduce volume by 40%. This isn’t optional — it’s the mechanism that allows the body to supercompensate, recover accumulated fatigue, and come back to the next training block at a higher baseline. People who refuse deloads don’t get more from their training. They get less, and they get it more slowly.

The deload is not a rest week. Intensity stays high. You are still training. You are reducing volume to let systemic recovery happen while maintaining the mechanical stimulus that tells the body to keep the muscle.

Volume Periodisation: Building the Wave

Most people who’ve been lifting for more than a year understand progressive overload. Fewer understand that the mechanism for applying it has to change as you advance. Adding weight every session stops working. Here’s what replaces it.

WHY LINEAR PROGRESSION STOPS WORKING

Linear progression — add weight every session, add weight every week — works for beginners because any stimulus produces an adaptation when the body hasn’t been trained. The nervous system efficiency improvements alone produce rapid strength gains in the first 6–12 months. The body is adapting to the stress of training itself.

As training age increases, the adaptation rate slows and the recovery requirement increases. A 5kg jump on the squat that took one week to recover from at six months training may take three weeks at three years training. Linear progression becomes impossible not because the adaptations aren’t happening, but because they’re happening more slowly than a linear model can accommodate.

WHAT PERIODISATION ACTUALLY IS

Periodisation is the systematic manipulation of training variables over time to manage fatigue and maximise adaptation. The basic principle: increase training stress progressively, then reduce it (deload), then increase again from a higher baseline. This creates a wave pattern where each peak is higher than the last.

The variables are: volume (total sets per muscle group), intensity (percentage of 1RM), and frequency (sessions per week). These can be manipulated across a mesocycle (4–6 weeks) or macrocycle (12–24 weeks). The simplest version: spend 3 weeks building volume, take 1 deload week, repeat with higher starting volume next block.

BUILDING THE WAVE IN PRACTICE

Identify your MV and MRV. Minimum Effective Volume (MEV) is the minimum volume needed to produce a training response. Maximum Recoverable Volume (MRV) is the most volume you can recover from. Start a block slightly above MEV. End the block approaching MRV. Deload. Begin the next block from an MEV higher than the previous block.

Use progressive overload within the block. Each week, add 1–2 sets per muscle group OR add load while holding sets constant. Don’t try to progress both variables simultaneously at intermediate-to-advanced level.

Track volume per muscle group, not just per session. If you’re doing 20 total sets per session for 3 muscles, each muscle is getting 6–7 sets per session. That’s actually quite low. The research suggests 10–20 sets per muscle group per week is the productive range for hypertrophy in intermediate+ lifters.

RECOMP APPLICATION

In my last recomp, average sets per session went from 11.2 (pre-recomp) to 19.7 (recomp). That’s a 75% volume increase applied systematically, not randomly. The split was structured to ensure each target muscle group was hit 2–3 times per week at sufficient volume. The results confirm the principle: volume drives hypertrophy when protein and consistency are in place.
